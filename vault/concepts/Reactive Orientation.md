# Reactive Orientation

The default operating mode of most people and organizations. Contrasted with the [[Creative Orientation]] in [[Structural Dynamics]].

In the reactive orientation, action is driven by problems. You identify what's wrong and try to fix it. This produces [[Oscillation]]: as the problem recedes, the motivation to act diminishes, and the underlying structure reasserts itself. Progress is followed by regression, followed by renewed effort.

## Why it fails structurally

The reactive orientation is not wrong because of lack of effort. It fails because the *structure* resolves toward equilibrium, not toward the desired outcome. When the problem is the motivation, reducing the problem reduces the motivation. This is a structural property, not a character flaw.

## How to recognize it

- "What's your biggest challenge?"
- "What keeps you up at night?"
- "What problems are you trying to solve?"

These are reactive framings. They center the problem, which means the problem is running the process.

## In the context of Statecraft Systems

The thesis document itself was initially drafted in a reactive frame ("The Problem") and had to be restructured around the [[Creative Orientation]] — starting from a declaration of what is being created, not a diagnosis of what is broken.
